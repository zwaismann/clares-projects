The Basics

What Is Detox?

Detoxification is the process of clearing a substance from your body while managing the symptoms of withdrawal. It's the necessary first step before any longer-term recovery can begin.

When your body becomes dependent on a substance like opiates or alcohol, it adapts to functioning with that substance present. When you stop, your body has to readjust. That readjustment is withdrawal, and it can range from deeply uncomfortable to medically dangerous.

Detox is not the same as treatment or recovery. It's the physical stabilization that makes treatment possible. Think of it as clearing the ground before you can build.

When to Seek Help

  • You need increasing amounts to feel the same effect
  • You feel sick or anxious when you stop using
  • You've tried to quit on your own and couldn't
  • Your use is affecting work, relationships, or health
  • You're using to avoid withdrawal, not to feel good
  • A loved one has expressed concern

If any of these resonate, you don't need to have "hit rock bottom." There is no threshold you have to cross to deserve help.

Opiate & Opioid Detox

What You Need to Know

What Are Opiates & Opioids?

Opioids include prescription painkillers (oxycodone, hydrocodone, codeine, morphine), heroin, and synthetic opioids like fentanyl. They work by binding to receptors in your brain that control pain and reward. Over time, your brain stops producing its own feel-good chemicals and relies on the drug instead.

When you stop, those receptors are left empty. That's what causes withdrawal.

Withdrawal Timeline

Phase 1
6 - 24 hours

Early Withdrawal

Anxiety, muscle aches, restlessness, insomnia, sweating, runny nose, excessive yawning. Often described as a severe flu coming on fast.

Phase 2
Days 1 - 3

Peak Symptoms

Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramping, rapid heartbeat, high blood pressure, dilated pupils. This is the hardest stretch. Symptoms are intense but temporary.

Phase 3
Days 4 - 7

Subsiding

Physical symptoms begin to ease. Fatigue, irritability, and trouble concentrating may linger. Sleep is often still disrupted. The worst is behind you.

Phase 4
Weeks 2+

Post-Acute

Some people experience extended symptoms - mood swings, low energy, cravings, sleep issues - that can last weeks to months. This is normal and manageable with support.

Types of Opiate Detox

Most Common

Medical Detox

Supervised in a clinical setting with medications to manage symptoms. Typically 5-7 days. Medications like clonidine, anti-nausea drugs, and sleep aids keep you as comfortable as possible.

Medication-Assisted

MAT Programs

Buprenorphine (Suboxone), methadone, or naltrexone used to ease withdrawal and reduce cravings. Can be short-term for detox or long-term for maintenance. Evidence-based and widely recommended.

Accelerated

Rapid Detox

Performed under sedation or anesthesia while medications flush opioids from your receptors. Compresses the withdrawal timeline to hours instead of days. Requires medical screening and monitoring.

Not Recommended

Cold Turkey

Stopping without medical support. While opiate withdrawal is rarely life-threatening, it's intensely uncomfortable and has the highest relapse rate. Relapse after tolerance drops is when overdose risk spikes.

Important: Opiate withdrawal is rarely fatal on its own, but the risk of relapse and subsequent overdose is very real. Medical supervision significantly improves both safety and success rates.

Alcohol Detox

Why Medical Supervision Matters

Alcohol Withdrawal Can Be Fatal

Unlike most substances, alcohol withdrawal can cause life-threatening complications including seizures and delirium tremens (DTs). If you are a heavy or long-term drinker, do not attempt to quit cold turkey without medical guidance.

Withdrawal Timeline

Phase 1
6 - 12 hours

Early Symptoms

Anxiety, hand tremors, nausea, insomnia, sweating, headache. Often begins within hours of the last drink. Many people mistake this for a hangover.

Phase 2
12 - 48 hours

Escalation

Increased blood pressure and heart rate, confusion, mild hallucinations, seizure risk begins. This is when medical monitoring becomes critical.

Phase 3
48 - 72 hours

Peak Danger

Risk of delirium tremens (DTs) - severe confusion, hallucinations, fever, seizures. DTs occur in roughly 5% of people withdrawing from alcohol but carry a mortality rate if untreated.

Phase 4
Days 5 - 7+

Stabilization

Acute symptoms resolve for most people. Sleep disturbance, anxiety, and mood instability can persist for weeks. Nutritional recovery - especially thiamine and B vitamins - is important during this phase.

Severity Levels

Mild

Occasional or moderate drinking with short history of dependence. Symptoms are uncomfortable but manageable. May be appropriate for outpatient detox with medical oversight.

Moderate

Daily drinking for months to years. Previous withdrawal episodes. Symptoms may include tremors, elevated vitals, and significant anxiety. Inpatient monitoring is strongly recommended.

Severe

Heavy daily drinking for years, previous seizures or DTs, co-occurring medical conditions. Requires inpatient medical detox. Benzodiazepines and anti-seizure medications are standard protocol.

What Medical Detox Looks Like

In a supervised setting, your vitals are monitored regularly and medications are administered on a schedule or as needed. The most common protocol uses benzodiazepines (like Librium or Ativan) on a tapering schedule to prevent seizures and keep you stable.

You'll also receive fluids, electrolytes, thiamine, folate, and other nutritional support. Many people are significantly malnourished by the time they enter detox, and physical recovery starts here.

The typical inpatient alcohol detox lasts 3-7 days depending on severity. After stabilization, the transition to a treatment program - whether inpatient rehab, outpatient counseling, or a support group - is the critical next step.

Detox alone does not treat addiction. It treats dependence. The work of understanding why you drink and building a life where you don't need to comes after.

Resources

Finding Help

What to Look for in a Detox Facility

Medical Staffing

24/7 nursing, a physician available at all times, and staff experienced in addiction medicine. Detox is a medical procedure - treat it like one.

Licensing & Accreditation

Look for state licensing and JCAHO or CARF accreditation. These ensure the facility meets established standards for safety and care.

Aftercare Planning

A good detox program starts planning your next step from day one. Whether it's residential treatment, outpatient therapy, or a support group, the transition matters.

Insurance & Cost

Most insurance plans cover medical detox under mental health and substance abuse benefits. Many facilities offer payment plans. Cost should not be a barrier to getting safe.

Supporting a Loved One

If someone you care about needs detox, you're already doing something right by learning about it. A few things to keep in mind:

  • You can't force someone into recovery, but you can make it easier for them to choose it
  • Express concern without ultimatums. "I'm worried about you" goes further than "you need to stop"
  • Offer to help with logistics - researching facilities, making calls, driving them there
  • Take care of yourself too. Al-Anon, Nar-Anon, and therapy for family members exist for a reason
  • Relapse is common and does not mean failure. Recovery is rarely a straight line

Free, 24/7 crisis support via text message. Trained counselors available anytime.

988
